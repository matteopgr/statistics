Fundamental Theorem of Calculus and Its Relationship with Density Functions and Cumulative Distribution Functions (CDFs)

The Fundamental Theorem of Calculus (FTC) provides a bridge between differentiation and integration, stating that if \( f(x) \) is continuous over an interval \( [a, b] \) and \( F(x) \) is an antiderivative of \( f(x) \) on that interval, then:

\[ F(x) = \int_a^x f(t) \, dt \]

and hence

\[ F'(x) = f(x). \]

This theorem can be applied to probability theory, where it plays a significant role in connecting a cumulative distribution function (CDF) and a probability density function (PDF).

In probability, if \( X \) is a continuous random variable with CDF \( F(x) \), which represents the probability that \( X \) takes a value less than or equal to \( x \), we define:

\[ F(x) = P(X \leq x) = \int_{-\infty}^x f(t) \, dt, \]

where \( f(x) \) is the probability density function (PDF) of \( X \).

By the FTC, differentiating \( F(x) \) with respect to \( x \) yields the PDF:

\[ f(x) = F'(x). \]

This shows that the PDF is the derivative of the CDF. Consequently, if we know the CDF of a continuous random variable, we can find its PDF by differentiation, and if we know the PDF, we can find the CDF by integration.
