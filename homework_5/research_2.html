Independence vs. Uncorrelation

Independence and uncorrelation are two fundamental concepts in probability and statistics that describe relationships between random variables, but they have different meanings and implications.

Independence

Two random variables \( X \) and \( Y \) are said to be independent if knowing the value of one variable provides no information about the value of the other. Mathematically, this is defined by:

\[ P(X = x \text{ and } Y = y) = P(X = x) P(Y = y), \]

or equivalently, for their joint probability density functions, \( f_{X,Y}(x, y) = f_X(x) f_Y(y) \). In this case, the variables are "free" from each other in the probabilistic sense, and knowing the distribution of one does not help in predicting the distribution of the other.

Uncorrelation

Two random variables \( X \) and \( Y \) are uncorrelated if their covariance is zero, which implies that there is no linear relationship between them. The covariance between \( X \) and \( Y \) is defined by:

\[ \operatorname{Cov}(X, Y) = \mathbb{E}[(X - \mathbb{E}[X])(Y - \mathbb{E}[Y])]. \]

If \( \operatorname{Cov}(X, Y) = 0 \), then \( X \) and \( Y \) are uncorrelated. The correlation coefficient \( \rho(X, Y) \), which is the normalized form of covariance, is also zero in this case:

\[ \rho(X, Y) = \frac{\operatorname{Cov}(X, Y)}{\sigma_X \sigma_Y} = 0, \]

where \( \sigma_X \) and \( \sigma_Y \) are the standard deviations of \( X \) and \( Y \), respectively.

Key Differences

  • Implications: Independence implies uncorrelation, but the reverse is not true. If \( X \) and \( Y \) are independent, then their covariance (and hence correlation) is zero. However, uncorrelated variables are not necessarily independent.
  • Linear vs. Non-linear Relationships: Uncorrelation only indicates the absence of a linear relationship between \( X \) and \( Y \). They may still have a non-linear dependency. For example, if \( Y = X^2 \), then \( X \) and \( Y \) are uncorrelated (assuming \( X \) is symmetric about zero), but they are clearly dependent because knowing \( X \) gives information about \( Y \).
  • Measures: Independence is measured by checking if the joint distribution factorizes into marginal distributions. Uncorrelation is measured by the covariance or correlation coefficient.
