Shannon Entropy and Diversity Measures of Distributions

Shannon entropy is a measure of the uncertainty or diversity in a probability distribution. For a discrete random variable \( X \) with possible outcomes \( \{x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_n\} \) and corresponding probabilities \( \{p_1, p_2, \ldots, p_n\} \), the entropy \( H(X) \) is defined as:

\[ H(X) = -\sum_{i=1}^n p_i \log_2 p_i \]

This quantifies the average amount of information or surprise associated with the outcomes of \( X \). Higher entropy corresponds to greater unpredictability, whereas lower entropy indicates more certainty in the distribution.

Other diversity measures include:

  • Gini Index: A measure of inequality in distributions.
  • Simpson's Diversity Index: The probability that two randomly selected items belong to the same category.
  • Effective Number of Species: Derived from entropy, representing the number of equally likely categories needed to achieve the observed diversity.

Primitive Root

A primitive root modulo \( p \), where \( p \) is a prime number, is a number \( g \) such that for every integer \( a \) coprime to \( p \), there exists an integer \( k \) satisfying:

\[ g^k \equiv a \mod p \]

Equivalently, \( g \) is a generator of the multiplicative group \( \mathbb{Z}_p^* \). This means the powers of \( g \) modulo \( p \) produce all integers from \( 1 \) to \( p-1 \).

For example, if \( p = 7 \), the primitive roots are \( g = 3 \) and \( g = 5 \), since their powers modulo \( 7 \) generate \( \{1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6\} \).
