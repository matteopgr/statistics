How It Works

  1. View the Function Differently:

    While the Riemann integral partitions the domain \([a, b]\) into subintervals, the Lebesgue integral partitions the range of the function \( f(x) \) into slices (level sets).

    Each slice corresponds to the set of all \(x\)-values where the function \( f(x) \) takes on a specific range of values.

  2. Compute Contributions of Slices:

    For each slice (value range of \(f(x)\)), calculate the "measure" (or probability in probabilistic contexts) of the corresponding set of \(x\)-values.

    Multiply this measure by the value of the function over the slice to get its contribution to the integral.

  3. Sum Up:

    The integral is the sum (or, technically, the limit of the sum) of these contributions:

    \[ \text{Lebesgue Integral} = \int f(x) \, d\mu(x) \]

    Here, \(\mu(x)\) represents the measure (e.g., length, area, or probability) of the set where \( f(x) \) has a specific range.

Strengths

  • Handles functions with discontinuities more robustly than the Riemann approach.
  • Can integrate functions that are unbounded or have singularities, as long as the "measure" remains finite.
  • Essential in modern probability and functional analysis, as it works seamlessly with probability density functions.

Limitations

  • More abstract and less intuitive than the Riemann integral.
  • Computationally more complex for practical applications, though often the two integrals yield the same result for well-behaved functions.
